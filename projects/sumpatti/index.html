The Problem

Finance feels like a foreign language

India has over 300 million adults entering the formal financial system for the first time. Most fintech apps assume users already understand SIPs, mutual funds, equity markets, and insurance products. They don’t.

Existing apps overwhelm new users with jargon, complex dashboards, and aggressive product-pushing. The result? 68% of users abandon onboarding before completing their first investment.

Sumpatti asked us: “How might we make personal finance feel as approachable as ordering food online?”

Financial Literacy Gap

Only 27% of Indian adults are financially literate (S&P Global FinLit Survey). Apps were built for the other 73%.

Intimidating Onboarding

KYC-heavy flows, PAN card requirements upfront, and zero context for why information is needed.

Product Fragmentation

Users need 4–5 different apps for insurance, SIPs, savings, and tax planning. No one offers the full basket.

Research

Listening to first-time investors

We conducted 24 in-depth interviews with first-time investors across Tier 1 and Tier 2 cities in India. We also ran contextual inquiry sessions where we watched users attempt onboarding on 4 competing fintech apps.

The patterns were consistent: users felt stupid, overwhelmed, and afraid of losing money. Not because they lacked intelligence — because the products lacked empathy.

Design Principles

Four pillars of the experience

01

Progressive Disclosure

Never show everything at once. Reveal complexity only when the user is ready. Start with goals, not products. Let curiosity drive exploration, not overwhelming menus.

02

Trust Before Transaction

Explain why before asking for what. Every data request comes with a plain-language reason. No dark patterns. No hidden fees. Transparency is the product.

03

Jargon-Free Language

Replace “diversified equity mutual fund” with “a basket of top companies.” Every financial term gets a plain-language tooltip. Education is embedded, not siloed.

04

Holistic by Default

Don’t just sell equities. Offer the full basket — insurance, savings, gold, SIPs, tax planning. Let users build a complete financial meal, not just a snack.

Key Flows

Designing the journey

Onboarding Wizard

A 5-step conversational onboarding that feels like chatting with a knowledgeable friend, not filling out a bank form. We ask about goals, comfort level, and timeline — then personalize the entire experience.

Step 1: “What are your goals?”

Step 2: “How do you feel about risk?”

Step 3: “Your personalized plan”

Risk Assessment

Traditional risk questionnaires are boring and confusing. We replaced checkboxes with relatable scenarios: “If your investment dropped 10% tomorrow, would you: (a) buy more, (b) wait it out, (c) sell immediately?” Visual sliders and friendly illustrations make it feel like a quiz, not a form.

Risk Assessment Flow — Interactive Scenarios

Portfolio Basket Builder

The core of Sumpatti — a drag-and-drop basket builder where users compose their financial meal. Each product is visualized as an ingredient: equities are the protein, bonds are the grains, insurance is the safety net, gold is the garnish. Users see their basket nutrition (diversification score) update in real time.

Basket Builder — Empty State

Basket Builder — Populated Portfolio

Education Hub

Bite-sized lessons (under 2 minutes each) that unlock as users explore new product categories. “What is a SIP?” appears the moment someone taps on SIPs for the first time. No homework, no prerequisites — just-in-time learning that builds confidence alongside portfolios.

Education Hub — Lesson Cards & Progress Tracker

Visual Language

Warm, approachable, clear

We chose a warm palette rooted in amber and soft cream — a deliberate departure from the cold blues and greens that dominate fintech. Finance should feel human, not corporate.

Illustrations use friendly, rounded characters in everyday settings — not boardrooms or stock tickers. Data visualization prioritizes clarity over cleverness: simple donut charts, clear progress bars, and color-coded categories that even a first-time investor can read at a glance.

Reflection

What I learned

This project taught me that the biggest design challenge in fintech isn’t information architecture or visual design — it’s emotional design. People don’t avoid investing because apps are ugly. They avoid it because they’re afraid.

The “basket of financial products” metaphor was the breakthrough. It reframed a complex portfolio into something familiar — a meal you compose, not a form you fill. That single metaphor shaped everything: the UI, the copy, the illustrations, even the onboarding flow.

If I could change one thing, I’d push harder on the education hub earlier. We shipped it as a V2 feature, but user feedback made it clear that embedded learning was core to the experience, not a nice-to-have.
