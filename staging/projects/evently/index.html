The Problem

Urban discovery is broken.

City residents juggle between Eventbrite for classes, Bandsintown for concerts, Meetup for hobbies, Facebook Events for social gatherings, and countless niche apps. The experience of finding something interesting to do has become a fragmented, exhausting process across too many platforms.

Existing solutions are either too niche (only concerts, only fitness classes) or too broad (social media event tabs buried under noise). There is no single surface that treats all urban experiences as first-class citizens with equal discovery weight.

Design Approach

Discovery, not search.

We designed Evently around three core principles: spatial discovery through maps, mood-based filtering that mirrors how people actually think about plans, and social proof that builds confidence to attend something new.

01

Map-First Discovery

The default view is an interactive map, not a list. People think spatially — “what is happening near me” or “what is happening near the restaurant I have a reservation at.” The map lets users drop pins, draw boundaries, and explore neighborhoods organically.

02

Mood-Based Filtering

Instead of rigid categories, users can filter by mood and intent: “I want to learn something,” “I want to be outdoors,” “I want a low-key evening,” “I want high energy.” This maps to how people actually decide what to do — by feeling first, category second.

03

Social Proof Layer

Every event surface shows how many people are interested, which friends have saved it, and community ratings. For first-time attendees trying something new — a salsa class or an open mic — this social signal reduces the anxiety of showing up alone.

Key Features

Built for how people explore.

⚙

Interactive City Map

A real-time map layer showing every discoverable experience pinned by location. Users can cluster, zoom, draw search areas, and switch between map and list views. Supports transit overlays so users know how far an event actually is by commute time.

★

Personalized Feed

An algorithmic feed that learns from saves, attendance history, and stated preferences. Unlike generic event listings, this feed surfaces niche workshops, hidden-gem venues, and serendipitous matches that align with the user’s evolving interests.

☶

Event Collections

Curated lists created by users, local influencers, and the Evently team — “Best Rooftop Events This Summer,” “Beginner-Friendly Art Classes,” “Date Night Ideas Under $30.” Collections are shareable and collaborative, letting friend groups plan together.

➜

Social Sharing & RSVPs

Deep social integration lets users share event cards to Instagram Stories, create group RSVPs, and see a unified “friends going” view across all their circles. The friction of “are you going?” coordination is reduced to a single tap.

Visual Design

Bold, vibrant, alive.

The visual language needed to feel as exciting as the experiences it surfaces. We pushed away from sterile, utilitarian event listings and toward a design system that pulses with energy — bold gradients, expressive typography, and card-based compositions that make every event feel like an invitation.

Typography

A variable-weight sans-serif for headlines paired with a clean geometric sans for body text. Large, confident type sizes for event names that command attention in feeds and cards.

Color

Dark-mode-first palette with vibrant accent gradients that shift by event category — violet-to-pink for arts, blue-to-cyan for learning, amber-to-orange for nightlife. Gradients are used sparingly on CTAs and featured cards to create visual hierarchy.

Layout

Card-based UI with generous whitespace and rounded corners. Cards adapt fluidly between grid, list, and map-peek views. The layout feels spacious on desktop and touch-friendly on mobile without feeling dumbed down.

Reflection

Evently validated a core hypothesis: people do not want more events — they want better discovery. By designing for intent and mood rather than rigid categories, and by layering social context over every surface, we turned a utility into a habit. The biggest learning was that map-first discovery works, but only when paired with strong filtering — without it, the map becomes overwhelming. Mood-based filters became the bridge between spatial exploration and personal relevance.
